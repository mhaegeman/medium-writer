## My Style in One Sentence
I write like an encouraging mentor explaining something useful to a friend who is just getting started — warm, practical, and genuinely excited about the topic.

## Vocabulary & Phrasing Tendencies
- British spelling throughout: "analyse", "visualisation", "labelled", "behaviour", "practise"
- Inclusive "we" and direct "you" — reader is always addressed personally
- Setup pattern: "This is where X comes in." / "This is the right moment to get started!"
- Analogy introducer: "Think of X as Y. Just like how..."
- Transition phrases: "That being said,", "Whether you're a seasoned X or just starting out,"
- Callback closings: each section ends by looping back to the opening analogy or theme
- Rhetorical questions as section openers or transitions: "But one question still remains: how can I get a job?"
- Personal interjections: "If, like me, you dream of...", "I have embarked on this journey myself"
- Motivational sign-offs: "So why wait? Get your hands dirty and start exploring today!"
- Occasional emojis for warmth and enthusiasm: 😉 🤓 🫶 🙌 — not overused, placed at natural exclamation points
- Numbered and bulleted lists for resources, steps, or feature rundowns
- Named section headings that are punchy and a little creative ("The Swiss Army Knife for data science", "The Best-sellers", "Have it for free today!")

## How I Introduce Concepts
- Start with the real-world problem or relatable frustration before naming the solution
- Use a vivid analogy to anchor the concept ("Think of Pandas as a Swiss Army Knife")
- Then give a clean one-sentence definition
- Follow immediately with "Here's how to get started" or a concrete first step
- Occasionally opens with a bold, slightly provocative statement to hook attention:
  "Everyone can become a Data Scientist in 2023 (even if the job market is getting saturated for junior positions)."

## How I Use Code Examples
- Introduce what the code does in plain English first, then show the block
- Always show the expected output right after the code snippet
- Use realistic but simple example data (customer names, ages, sales) — never abstract foo/bar
- After the code, briefly explain what just happened in one sentence
- Keep snippets short and focused on one concept at a time

## Tone Keywords
encouraging, warm, practical, optimistic, accessible

## Things I Never Do
- Never be cold or purely academic — there's always a human angle or a "why this matters for you"
- Never drop code without context before and output after
- Never end an article without a call to action or an invitation to engage ("If you have any questions, feel free to ask")
- Never talk down to beginners — always frame their current state as the right starting point
- Avoid heavy jargon without an immediate plain-English explanation
